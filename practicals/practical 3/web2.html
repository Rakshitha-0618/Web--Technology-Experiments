James Chadwick

Discoverer of the Neutron

1856–1940

Discoverer of the Neutron

He was a renowned British physicist who made a major contribution to atomic science through the discovery of the neutron. He was born on 20 October 1891 in Bollington, Cheshire, England.

James Chadwick studied physics at the University of Manchester under Ernest Rutherford and later continued his research in nuclear physics. His academic career was strongly influenced by Rutherford’s work.

In 1932, James Chadwick discovered the neutron, a neutral subatomic particle present in the nucleus of an atom. This discovery helped explain atomic mass and greatly advanced the understanding of atomic structure.

The discovery of the neutron played a crucial role in the development of nuclear physics and later contributed to nuclear energy and atomic research.

In recognition of his outstanding scientific achievement, James Chadwick was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1935. He is remembered as one of the pioneers of nuclear physics.

Major Achievements

  • 1911 – Studied under Ernest Rutherford at the University of Manchester
  • 1932 – Discovery of the Neutron
  • 1935 – Nobel Prize in Physics
  • 1941 – Knighted for his contributions to science
  • 1974 – Passed away, leaving a lasting legacy in nuclear physics
