J.J. THOMSON

1856–1940

J. J. Thomson is known as the discoverer of the electron and a pioneer who laid the foundation of modern atomic and electron physics

J. J. Thomson was a British physicist who discovered the electron in 1897 through cathode ray experiments, proving that atoms are not indivisible; his work laid the foundation for modern atomic theory and electron physics and led to the proposal of the plum pudding model of the atom.

Father of electron physics

Joseph John Thomson, popularly known as J. J. Thomson, was a renowned British physicist who made groundbreaking contributions to atomic science. He was born on 18 December 1856 in Cheetham Hill, Manchester, England. From an early age, he showed exceptional ability in mathematics and science, which guided him toward a career in physics.

Thomson began his education at Owens College, Manchester, where he initially studied engineering. Later, he moved to Trinity College, Cambridge, and specialized in physics and mathematics. Due to his remarkable academic talent, he gained recognition very early in his career. In 1884, at the age of just 28, he was appointed the Cavendish Professor of Experimental Physics at the University of Cambridge, one of the most prestigious positions in the field of physics.

J. J. Thomson’s most significant achievement came in 1897, when he discovered the electron through his experiments with cathode rays. This discovery proved that atoms are not indivisible particles, as previously believed, but are made up of smaller subatomic particles. This finding revolutionized the understanding of atomic structure and marked the beginning of modern atomic physics.

After discovering the electron, Thomson proposed the Plum Pudding Model of the atom in 1904. According to this model, an atom consists of a positively charged sphere with negatively charged electrons embedded within it. Although this model was later replaced by more accurate atomic models, it was an important step in the development of atomic theory.

For his outstanding contributions to science, J. J. Thomson was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1906 for his research on the conduction of electricity in gases. In 1908, he was knighted by the British government, and from then on he was known as Sir J. J. Thomson. He is also remembered as the Father of Electron Physics because of his pioneering work on electrons.

Apart from his discoveries, Thomson was a great teacher and mentor. Many of his students later became famous scientists, including Ernest Rutherford, who discovered the atomic nucleus. In 1913, Thomson discovered the existence of isotopes and contributed to the development of the mass spectrograph, laying the foundation for mass spectrometry.

In 1918, he became the Master of Trinity College, Cambridge, a position he held for several years. After a long and distinguished career in science and education, J. J. Thomson passed away on 30 August 1940. He was buried in Westminster Abbey, a rare honor given to great scientists.

J. J. Thomson’s contributions transformed the field of physics and opened the door to the study of subatomic particles. His discovery of the electron remains one of the most important milestones in the history of science.

  • 1884 – Became the Cavendish Professor of Experimental Physics at the University of Cambridge.
  • 1897 – Discovered the electron through cathode ray experiments, proving atoms are divisible.

  • 1904 – Proposed the Plum Pudding Model of the atom, explaining atomic structure.
  • 1906 – Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for his research on the conduction of electricity in gases.
  • 1908 – Knighted by the British Government, after which he was known as Sir J. J. Thomson.
  • 1913 – Discovered isotopes by studying positive rays.
  • 1913 – Developed early work that led to the mass spectrograph, laying the foundation of mass spectrometry.
  • 1918 – Became Master of Trinity College, Cambridge, a prestigious academic position.
  • 1937 – Retired from academic life after decades of contribution to science.
  • 1940 – Passed away, leaving a lasting legacy in modern atomic and electron physics.
